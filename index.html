Analyzing the Effects of Cognitive Biases on the Accuracies of Microlaborers

Crowdsourcing information on microlabor platforms is integral to many ML projects. For example, if one needed a large amount of labeled data (e.g. image classification), this data can be split and assigned to hundreds of workers for a relatively low cost. However, the nature of how tasks are designed and the inherent biases held by microlaborers can affect the accuracies of the tasks. We did a case study observing the effect of overconfidence cognitive biases and anchoring cognitive biases on the accuracies of labeling satirical and non-satirical texts and generated and non-generated satirical texts from microlaborers on Amazon Turker. Artificial Intelligent Ultimate Tic-Tac-Toe Agent

Ultimate tic-tac-toe is a board game that contains nine grids of traditional tic-toe-toe games to make a global game. In order to win ultimate tic-tac-toe, the players must win the global board by winning three local games in a row, column, or diagonal. Since the global game is indirectly affected by the local game, the complexity of the game is harder than that of traditional tic-tac-toe. Ultimate tic-tac-toe is an unsolved problem, unlike similar board games such as checkers and Go. We used multiple AI algorithms agents to simulate games to see which algorithm had the best chances of winning the game. The two key agents were a minimax algorithm and a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithm. We used three heuristic functions for the minimax algorithms to test different facets of the game (local games versus global games, defensive versus offensive, etc.). We also implemented depth-bounded MCTS and alpha-beta pruning for minimax players for computational reasons. See our presentation below!
